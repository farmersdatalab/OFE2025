Welcome to your 2025 farm report. This report summarizes the on-farm nitrogen experiment conducted in your field, where cover crop strips (treatment) were compared against no-cover-crop controls.

Take-home message: Across all four nitrogen indicators (NBI, Chlorophyll, Biomass %N, and CSNT) corn plants in the cover crop strips consistently showed better nitrogen status than those in the control strips. Leaf readings were greener and more nitrogen-sufficient, plant tissue had noticeably more nitrogen, and end-of-season stalk nitrate revealed a clear difference between treatments: control stalks were well below the sufficiency threshold, while cover crop stalks were within the optimal range. Your soil health score is solid overall, but building biological activity through continued cover cropping is the clearest path to unlocking even more nitrogen from the soil over time.

🌿 Nitrogen Balance Index (NBI)

The Nitrogen Balance Index (NBI) is measured with the Dualex sensor and reflects the ratio of chlorophyll to flavonoids in the leaf. A higher NBI indicates better nitrogen status, the plant has enough N to build chlorophyll without accumulating excess flavonoids as a stress response. e took two readings per plant and averaged them, then compared the cover crop strips against the control strips to see if the cover crop made a difference using a two-sample t-test.The result was statistically significant, meaning the difference we saw is real and not just random field variation.

🍃 Chlorophyll (Chl)

The Chlorophyll Index (Chl) is also measured with the Dualex sensor and estimates the amount of chlorophyll in the leaf. Chlorophyll is directly linked to the plant’s capacity for photosynthesis and is a reliable proxy for nitrogen sufficiency. Higher values indicate greener, more nitrogen-sufficient leaves. As with NBI, two leaves per plant were averaged and treatments compared with a t-test.

🌽 Cornstalk Nitrate Test (CSNT)

The Cornstalk Nitrate Test (CSNT) measures nitrate concentration in the lower cornstalk at the end of the season. It is a post-harvest diagnostic tool: values below 250 ppm indicate insufficient nitrogen, while the optimal range is between 750 and 2000 ppm. Because only one sample per treatment was collected, no statistical comparison is possible, this is a field-level observation.

🌱 Soil Health Assessment

Soil health was assessed using the Cornell Soil Health Test (sampled 06/11/2025, Field 3). The overall quality score was 69/100 (High). The physical structure of your soil is strong: water holding capacity and surface hardness both came back well. The area to watch is biological activity, soil respiration and active carbon scored low, which means the microbial community that breaks down organic matter and cycles nutrients isn’t as active as it could be. The good news is that cover cropping directly targets exactly this: feeding soil microbes, adding carbon, and building the biological engine of your soil over time.

📝 Conclusions

🌿 Leaf Nitrogen Status (NBI)
Cover crop strips showed a 12.8% higher NBI compared to the control (76 vs 67.4). That means corn plants growing in the cover crop strips had significantly better nitrogen status mid-season, a clear and statistically meaningful difference.
🍃 Chlorophyll Index (Chl)
Chlorophyll values were also higher in cover crop plots (+6.6%), consistent with the NBI result. Higher chlorophyll reflects greener, more nitrogen-sufficient leaves, a direct sign of improved N uptake in the treatment strips.
🧪 Biomass Nitrogen (%N)
Laboratory analysis of corn biomass showed treatment plants had +17.6% more nitrogen in their tissue (2.286% vs 1.944%). The effect size (Cohens d = -0.57) suggests a meaningful biological difference, although the small sample size prevents formal statistical testing.
🌽 End-of-Season CSNT
The stalk nitrate result is the most striking finding of the season. The control came in at just 79 ppm, well below the 250 ppm sufficiency threshold, indicating the crop ran short on nitrogen late in the season. The cover crop strip came in at 938 ppm, right in the optimal range (750–2,000 ppm). The cover crop appears to have kept nitrogen flowing to the plant all the way through harvest.
🌱 Soil Health
Your overall soil health score of 69/100 (High) reflects good physical structure but low biological activity, particularly soil respiration (score 16) and active carbon (score 34). Continued cover cropping is the most direct way to address both: more organic inputs mean more microbial activity, which means more nitrogen cycling from the soil itself over time.
