2025 Cropping Season Field 302-3  |  Fixed rates: 25–62 lb N/ac  |  Variable-rate: R0, R1, R6, R9  |  Research question: Do cover crops and improved soil health compensate for reductions in applied nitrogen when it comes to grain yield?
⬇ Download PDF

Welcome to your 2025 farm report. This report summarizes the on-farm nitrogen rate experiment conducted in field 302-3. The entire field has cover crop. The field contains 10 N rate strips (R0–R9): strips R2, R3, R4, R5, R7, and R8 received fixed N rates (25–62 lb N/ac), while strips R0, R1, R6, and R9 received variable rates. The “Control” label in the data refers to the area of the field with variable N rate (avg 30 lb/ac). Variable-rate strips are presented as a single “Variable” group, allowing a direct comparison of fixed vs. variable-rate N management.

Take-home message: Across all nitrogen indicators — Biomass %N, CSNT, and corn yield — the data suggests a consistent response to N rate: higher applied nitrogen was associated with greater tissue N and end-of-season stalk nitrate. However, yield did not follow the same pattern. The 50 lb N/ac strip outperformed the higher 62 lb N/ac rate, suggesting that additional nitrogen beyond a certain point does not translate into more grain. Variable-rate strips showed the lowest yield and stalk nitrate values just above the sufficiency threshold, indicating N may have been the limiting factor in those areas. With soil chemistry in excellent condition but biological activity still developing, continued cover cropping could gradually reduce dependence on applied N.

🌽 Cornstalk Nitrate Test (CSNT)

The Cornstalk Nitrate Test (CSNT) measures nitrate concentration in the lower cornstalk at the end of the season. It is a post-harvest diagnostic tool: values below 250 ppm indicate insufficient nitrogen, while the optimal range is between 750 and 2000 ppm. Each strip had two samples (East = E, West = W positions), and their average is used as the plot value.

🌱 Soil Health Assessment

Soil health was assessed using the Cornell Soil Health Test. The overall quality score was 65/100 (High). Chemical indicators (pH, P, K, additional nutrients) all scored 100 - ideal. Key opportunities for improvement are in biological indicators: Organic Matter (score 22 - Low), Soil Respiration (score 22 - Low), and Predicted Soil Protein (score 37 - Low). These suggest that microbial activity and organic matter are limiting long-term soil function, which is directly relevant to the potential for internal nitrogen supply from cover crop residues.

📝 Conclusions

🌱 Cover Crop Biomass
The cover crop averaged 2.28% N. A C:N ratio below ~25 indicates the cover crop should release nitrogen relatively quickly during decomposition, potentially contributing to early-season corn N supply.
🧪 Biomass %N in Corn
Across N rate groups, tissue nitrogen ranged from 3.57% (25 lb N/ac) to 4.17% (62 lb N/ac). The higher rates tended to show higher tissue N, though with only 1–2 plots per group, formal statistical testing is not appropriate.
🌽 Cornstalk Nitrate (CSNT)
End-of-season stalk nitrate increased with N rate: from 336 ppm (25 lb N/ac) to 943 ppm (62 lb N/ac). All fixed-rate strips are above the 250 ppm deficiency threshold. Variable-rate strips averaged 257 ppm, just above the threshold.
🌾 Corn Yield
The 50 lb N/ac strip produced the highest mean yield at 178.1 bu/ac, outperforming both the higher rate (62 lb N/ac = 170.8 bu/ac) and the variable-rate strips (155.7 bu/ac). This suggests that 50 lb N/ac may be near the economic optimum under these field conditions.
🌱 Soil Health
The overall soil health score of 65/100 (High) reflects excellent chemical status but low biological activity. Continued cover cropping could improve organic matter and microbial activity over time, potentially reducing the need for applied N in future seasons.
