Welcome to your 2025 farm report. This report summarizes the on-farm nitrogen experiment conducted in your Kolb2 field, where clover cover crop strips (treatment) were compared against no-cover-crop controls. The central question this season: can a clover cover crop supply 60 lbs of nitrogen per acre to the following corn crop?

Take-home message: This season’s data provides multiple windows into nitrogen dynamics across the clover-treated and control strips. The in-season Dualex readings, corn biomass nitrogen, grain nitrogen content, and end-of-season cornstalk nitrate test together help evaluate whether the clover cover crop was able to meet the 60 lb N/ac target. Both CSNT values came in below the 250 ppm sufficiency threshold, suggesting nitrogen was limiting across the field regardless of treatment, an important finding in itself.

🌿 Nitrogen Balance Index (NBI)

The Nitrogen Balance Index (NBI) is measured with the Dualex sensor and reflects the ratio of chlorophyll to flavonoids in the leaf. A higher NBI indicates better nitrogen status: the plant has enough N to build chlorophyll without accumulating excess flavonoids as a stress response. Two leaves per plant were measured and averaged. Results are compared between Control (no cover crop) and Treatment (clover cover crop) strips using a two-sample t-test.

🍃 Chlorophyll (Chl)

The Chlorophyll Index (Chl) is also measured with the Dualex sensor and estimates the amount of chlorophyll in the leaf. Chlorophyll is directly linked to the plant’s capacity for photosynthesis and is a reliable proxy for nitrogen sufficiency. Higher values indicate greener, more nitrogen-sufficient leaves. 🌽 Cornstalk Nitrate Test (CSNT)

The Cornstalk Nitrate Test (CSNT) measures nitrate concentration in the lower cornstalk at the end of the season. It is a post-harvest diagnostic tool: values above 250 ppm suggest the crop had sufficient nitrogen; values below 250 ppm suggest nitrogen was limiting. For Kolb2 field, both the control and cover treatment fell below 250 ppm, indicating that nitrogen was limiting in both strips. Notably, the cover treatment measured lower than the control (57 vs 74 ppm), though both are in the same deficient range. Because only one sample per treatment was collected, no statistical comparison is possible — this is a field-level observation.

🌱 Soil Health Assessment

Soil health was assessed using the Cornell Soil Health Test. The overall quality score was 78/100 (High). Key items to watch: Soil pH scored 28 (Low, orange) at 5.7, below the optimal 6.2 - 6.8 range for corn, which can reduce nutrient availability and should be addressed with lime. Soil Respiration (score 56) and Active Carbon (score 54) are both in the Medium range, indicating microbial activity and labile carbon could improve. Physical and most biological indicators are strong: Aggregate Stability (100), Organic Matter (100), and AWC (93) reflect the benefits of no-till management. Aluminum (105.1 ppm) is flagged as excessive, which is linked to the low pH.

📝 Conclusion

🌿 Leaf Nitrogen Status (NBI)
Cover crop plots showed a 16% difference in NBI compared to control (89.2 vs 76.9, p = 0.00118). Chlorophyll also differed by 12.6% (p < 0.001). These in-season readings capture the nitrogen status.
🧪 Biomass Nitrogen (%N)
Laboratory analysis of corn biomass showed cover plots had +18.2% difference in tissue nitrogen (2.828% vs 2.392%). Cohen's d = -1.53. Small sample size (n = 5 vs 5) — descriptive only.
🌾 Grain Nitrogen (%N)
At harvest, grain %N was +16.3% different in cover strips (1.262% vs 1.085%, p = 0.00729, n = 10 vs 20). Grain N reflects the full-season nitrogen story, integrating both applied fertilizer and any cover crop contribution.
🌽 CSNT — N Limiting in Both Strips
End-of-season CSNT values were below 250 ppm in both treatments (Control: 74 ppm; Cover: 57 ppm). This indicates the corn crop was nitrogen-limited regardless of the cover crop treatment. The 60 lb/ac clover N target was not demonstrably met this season — direct clover biomass sampling is needed to quantify actual N supply in 2026.
